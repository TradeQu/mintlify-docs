## What fired

MeshQu is raising **critical-severity decision alerts** (the `ALERT` outcome / critical-severity alert events) faster than usual.

| Alert | Condition | For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `MeshQuCriticalAlertSpike` (warning) | critical alerts generated > **1/sec** | 5 min |

This is a **business-logic** signal. It reflects what tenant policies are flagging, not an infrastructure fault. A surge of critical alerts may itself indicate something real happening in a tenant's traffic.

## Symptoms

- The critical-alert-generation panel is above the threshold line.
- Tenants subscribed to alert webhooks receive a burst of notifications — a [webhook-backlog](/runbooks/webhook-backlog) may build downstream.

## First actions

1. **This is not a MeshQu fault.** A spike means policies are firing critical alerts. That can be correct (real anomaly in tenant traffic) or a policy misconfiguration.
2. **Scope it.** One tenant, one policy, one decision type? Concentrate the investigation where the spike originates.
3. **Correlate with policy changes.** A newly activated policy or a changed alert rule can produce a sudden surge. Check the policy lifecycle timeline.
4. **Assess downstream impact.** A burst of alerts drives a burst of webhooks — check [webhook-backlog](/runbooks/webhook-backlog) and [webhook-failures](/runbooks/webhook-failures).
5. **Notify, don't suppress.** If the alerts are legitimate, the tenant needs to know. If they are noise from a misconfigured rule, that is a policy fix the tenant owns — do not silence alerts to clear the signal.
